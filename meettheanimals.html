Meet the Animals of the Wetlands

Invertebrates are animals without a backbone. Wetlands are home to many fascinating ones like insects, snails, and crustaceans!

Dragonfly

Dragonflies are one of the most successful hunters in the animal kingdom!

Apple Snail

Florida Apple Snails make a delicous snack for Limpkins and Florida snail kites! It is also Florida's largest freshwater snail!

Shinyrayed pocketbook

I am very sensitive to changes in the water, which means I can be an indicator of a healthy ecosystem!

Mosquito

I can be dangerous by carrying diseases to people, but I'm also an important food source for all kinds of different animals!

Grass Shrimp

I clean up by eating algea, dead plants and animals. I'm also a tasty snack for lots of different animals! So I can't stay still long!

There are over 200 species of known freshwater fish in Florida. They are ectothermic meaning cold-blooded, meaning they rely on the environment around them for body heat. Florida is recognized as the top state in the U.S. for freshwater fishing!

Largemouth Bass

I'm the most popular sports fish in North American, and I'm the state fish of Florida.

Channel Catfish

My whiskers aren't just for show, I use them to help find prey in murky water!

Bluegill

They call me a pan fish! Why? Cause I'm delicous when fried in a pan!

Amphibian means "double life", which means that most of us spend a part of our life in the water and a part of our life on land! We absorb water and gases through our skin, which makes us excellent indicators of habitat quality!

Southern Leopard Frog

I am a strong jumper, able to get away from predators with a mighty hop! I will also call out most of the year, but you can hear me loudest during the winter and early spring.

Tiger Salamander

I'm Florida's largest terrestrial salamander! I can often be found in other animals burrows.

Two-toed Amphiuma

I sometimes get confused for an eel, but I'm not. I love muddy waters and I'm an excellent predator! I have powerful jaws and sharp teeth, so look out for my bite!

Reptiles are also ectothermic, having scales and leathery skin. Snakes make up 50% of all native reptiles in Florida!

Cottonmouth

Yes, I'm venomous! I use my venom to hunt for prey, only when I'm desperate will I use it in self defense. I also don't chase people, I am much more likely to get in a defensive pose and show you why they call me cottonmouth!

Snapping Turtle

Yes, I'm venomous! I use my venom to hunt for prey, only when I'm desperate will I use it in self defense. I also don't chase people, I am much more likely to get in a defensive pose and show you why they call me cottonmouth!

American Alligator

I am a large apex predator, which means I'm at the top of the food web! I'm also a key-stone species, which means without me the ecosystem could be severly impacted. I dig depressions into wetlands that provide a watering hole for many animals during dry seasons!

Birds are Endothermic, meaning they produce their own body heat. They have wings, feathers and beaks or bills. While all birds have wings, not all birds can fly. Some are great at swimming though!

Blue Heron

I am a large predatory bird with a sharp bill. I wade though the water in search for fish and will remain motionless for a long time to let them get close

Wood Duck

I am a large predatory bird with a sharp bill. I wade though the water in search for fish and will remain motionless for a long time to let them get close

Florida Snail Kite

I am a raptor, I am a predtory bird with sharp talons and beak. I eat almost exclusively apple snails! But the native apple snails have to fight for space with invasive species it spell bad news for me!

Mammals are warm-blooded with fur or hair! They produce milk for their young and live in a wide variety of habitats!

Beaver

I'm an ecological engineer! I build dams that block the flow of water and can create ponds for my family to live in.

Manatee

I'm a fully aquatic mammal closely related to elephants. During the winter you will often find us heading up stream to hang out in the springs for the warmer waters.

River Otter

I swim gracefully and playfully though the water, often in search for my next meal. I eat mussels, crayfish, small reptiles, fish and more. I usually travel with a partner or with my family, we have thick oiled fur to help with swimming and keeping us warm.
